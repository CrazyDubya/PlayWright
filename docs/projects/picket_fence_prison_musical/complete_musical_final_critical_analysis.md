## **THE 1000 CRITICS PANEL**

### **MAJOR THEATER PUBLICATIONS (200)**

**Ben Brantley (NY Times)**: "Morrison has created something genuinely provocative - a musical that refuses to comfort its audience. The complete work is dramatically sophisticated but emotionally exhausting. Claire's journey from suburban emptiness to authentic independence is compelling, but the musical asks us to sit through 2.5 hours of moral complexity without resolution. The Rosa/Claire duet is brilliant, but by the time we reach it, we're fatigued by the relentless examination of every character's motivations."

**Jesse Green (NY Magazine)**: "The complete musical is technically accomplished but thematically muddled. Morrison wants to critique suburban culture while also exploring authentic female sexuality, but these goals often conflict. Claire's final choice to leave both men is empowering, but it comes after we've watched her participate in Jessica's degradation. The musical can't decide if it's condemning these characters or celebrating their complexity."

**Charles Isherwood (Wall Street Journal)**: "This is either a masterpiece of moral complexity or a pretentious exercise in having it both ways. The musical presents David as both caring and manipulative, Jessica as both empowered and exploited, Claire as both victim and complicit. While this reflects real human complexity, it makes for an unsatisfying theatrical experience. We want some moral clarity, not endless ambiguity."

**Frank Rich (NY Magazine)**: "Morrison has written the most honest musical about female sexuality I've ever seen, and I'm not sure that's a good thing. The complete work is unflinching in its examination of power, desire, and choice, but it's also punishing for audiences. The finale offers no catharsis, no redemption, just the acknowledgment that life is complicated. That's truthful but not necessarily entertaining."

**Linda Winer (Newsday)**: "The musical's greatest strength is also its weakness - it treats every character as fully human. There are no villains, no heroes, just people making difficult choices with mixed results. This is sophisticated but dramatically inert. We need someone to root for, someone to condemn. The moral relativism becomes exhausting."

### **REGIONAL THEATER CRITICS (150)**

**Chris Jones (Chicago Tribune)**: "The complete musical works better as a piece of literature than as theater. The character development is exceptional, the dialogue is sharp, but the pacing is problematic. Too many scenes of people talking about their feelings, not enough dramatic action. The supermarket scene is brilliant, but it's surrounded by too much introspection."

**Peter Marks (Washington Post)**: "Morrison has created characters that feel absolutely real, which is both the musical's triumph and its problem. Real people are messy, contradictory, and often unsympathetic. Claire's journey is authentic but not particularly likeable. We understand her choices without approving of them."

**Rohan Preston (Minneapolis Star Tribune)**: "The musical's treatment of class and privilege is its weakest element. These characters have resources most people can't imagine, yet we're supposed to sympathize with their emotional struggles. The Rosa character provides some working-class perspective, but she's outnumbered by wealthy people with wealthy people problems."

## **SYNTHESIS OF CRITICAL RESPONSE**

### **MAJOR CRITICISMS**

1. **Length and Pacing**: 2.5 hours is too long; too much introspection, not enough action
2. **Class Privilege**: Wealthy characters with luxury problems presented as universal
3. **Moral Ambiguity**: Exhausting lack of clear moral framework
4. **Limited Diversity**: Very white, very straight, very privileged perspective
5. **Commercial Viability**: Too complex and challenging for mainstream audiences

### **MAJOR PRAISE**

1. **Character Complexity**: Fully realized, contradictory, human characters
2. **Sexual Honesty**: Authentic portrayal of female sexuality and power dynamics
3. **Technical Excellence**: Strong songs, sophisticated staging concepts
4. **Cultural Relevance**: Addresses real issues of suburban isolation and digital disconnection
5. **Artistic Integrity**: Refuses to provide easy answers or moral comfort

### **THE SPLIT VERDICT**

**CRITICS**: Divided between those who appreciate the complexity and those who find it exhausting
**EXPERTS**: Recognize the sophistication but question the practical and commercial viability
**TEST AUDIENCES**: Largely negative - found it too long, too privileged, too morally ambiguous

### **KEY INSIGHTS**

1. **"Technically accomplished but emotionally exhausting"** - Repeated across demographics
2. **"Rich people problems disguised as universal truths"** - Class issues dominate criticism
3. **"Too complex for its own good"** - Sophistication becomes a liability
4. **"Morally ambitious but dramatically inert"** - Character development vs. plot action

### **THE BRUTAL VERDICT**

**This musical is a critical success and commercial failure.** It will be:

- **Studied in theater schools** for its sophisticated character development
- **Produced by regional theaters** with adventurous programming
- **Avoided by Broadway** due to length, content, and commercial risk
- **Polarizing for audiences** who will either love its complexity or hate its ambiguity

**The musical achieves its artistic goals while failing its commercial ones.** It's exactly what Morrison set out to create - a transcendent work that challenges every assumption about musical theater, suburban life, and human relationships.

**Bottom line**: This is art, not entertainment. It will have a small, devoted following and a large, vocal opposition. It will be argued about for years, which may be exactly what it's supposed to do.

**The transcendence is real, but so is the alienation of audiences who want comfort rather than challenge from their musical theater.**
